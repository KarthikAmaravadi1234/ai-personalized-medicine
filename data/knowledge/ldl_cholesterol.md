# LDL Cholesterol

Source: Adapted from CDC and NIH public health education materials (educational summary).

LDL (low-density lipoprotein) cholesterol is often called "bad" cholesterol. When
there is too much LDL in the blood, it can build up on the walls of arteries as
plaque. Over time this buildup, called atherosclerosis, narrows the arteries and
raises the risk of heart attack and stroke.

## What the numbers mean

LDL cholesterol is measured in milligrams per deciliter (mg/dL). General reference
ranges used for adults are:

- Optimal: less than 100 mg/dL
- Near optimal: 100 to 129 mg/dL
- Borderline high: 130 to 159 mg/dL
- High: 160 to 189 mg/dL
- Very high: 190 mg/dL and above

People who already have cardiovascular disease or diabetes are often advised to aim
for lower LDL targets.

## Lowering LDL

Lifestyle steps that help lower LDL include eating less saturated and trans fat,
increasing soluble fiber, exercising regularly, losing excess weight, and quitting
smoking. When lifestyle changes are not enough, clinicians may prescribe statins or
other cholesterol-lowering medicines.
